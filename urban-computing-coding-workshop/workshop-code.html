What are heat risks associated with climate change?
Climate change is intensifying heat risks by raising average temperatures, increasing the frequency and duration of heat waves, and causing nights to stay hotter for longer. These harsher heat conditions strain buildings and public health, especially in dense cities where the urban heat island effect further elevates temperatures. As cooling systems become overwhelmed and indoor environments heat up, residents face higher risks of heat exhaustion, dehydration, and other heat-related illnesses. Strengthening resilience through better insulation, natural ventilation, shading, reflective surfaces, and reliable cooling systems is essential to protect vulnerable populations.
How is this relevant to NYCHA?
This is especially relevant to NYCHA because many developments already suffer from poor insulation, aging facades, and outdated cooling systems that cannot handle rising temperatures. NYCHA residents are disproportionately low-income, elderly, or living with health conditions that make them more vulnerable to extreme heat. Many households cannot afford efficient air-conditioning or cooling upgrades, leaving them reliant on aging building systems that are easily overwhelmed during heat waves. As temperatures rise, these inequities compound, making overheating in NYCHA building a critical public health and environmental justice concern.

What are flood risks associated with climate change?
Climate change is increasing flood risks worldwide through sea level rise, which causes higher coastal flooding, and more intense precipitation events, which lead to urban and riverine flooding. Extreme storms and changing weather patterns also increase the frequency and severity of floods, threatening infrastructure, communities, and ecosystems. Adaptation strategies include resilient building design, green infrastructure, and improved stormwater management.
How is this relevant to NYCHA?
NYCHA developments in New York City are vulnerable to several types of flood risk, primarily coastal flooding from storm surges during hurricanes or nor’easters, pluvial flooding from heavy rainfall overwhelming drainage systems, and fluvial or riverine flooding in areas near waterways. These floods can damage building infrastructure, disrupt utilities, and pose health and safety risks to residents. Mitigation often involves both structural measures, like flood barriers and raised mechanical systems, and landscape-based solutions, like bioswales and detention areas.

Research Statement
Our research focuses on the climate resilience of NYCHA developments, specifically examining how existing public
housing can better withstand thermal stress and flood risks. The study aims to identify vulnerabilities that make
NYCHA housing susceptible to heat exposure and flooding. It will develop design recommendations and retrofit
strategies that improve thermal comfort and flood resilience, ensuring safer and more sustainable living conditions
for residents in the face of climate change.
Relevance
This research addresses critical issues of climate vulnerability, social equity, and sustainable design within New York City’s public housing system. NYCHA developments house some of the city’s most climate-vulnerable populations. Rising temperatures, intensifying heat waves, and increased flood risk disproportionately affect these communities, revealing a systemic gap in how design and policy account for resilience in existing urban housing.
Contributions
The project delivers a climate-informed classification of NYCHA developments based on daylight adequacy and
thermal exposure. By mapping sun access, shadow patterns, and flood vulnerability, it visualizes where overheating,
poor daylight, or flooding intersect.

From these findings, the research develops targeted design and retrofit strategies, for example, adding shading or
reflective surfaces for overheated façades, elevating ground-floor functions in flood zones, or implementing green
spaces on the sites themselves. Ultimately, the project contributes to architectural practice by demonstrating how
climate data can directly inform equitable, site-specific design responses, offering a replicable model for resilient
public housing retrofits in other urban contexts.
